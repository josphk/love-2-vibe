# Game Business Domain Map

A curated vocabulary reference for the commercial side of making and selling games. Use these terms to prompt AI tools with specificity — each entry includes a plain-language definition and an example prompt showing the term in context.

**How to use this page:** Scan the branch that matches your business problem. Grab the precise term, drop it into your prompt, and get better results than vague descriptions ever produce.

## Revenue & Monetization

How games make money — pricing, in-game purchases, and post-launch revenue.

### Pricing Models

How you set the price tag — or decide not to have one.

- **Premium** — The traditional model: pay once, get the whole game. No strings attached. Works best when the game delivers a complete experience at launch.
  *"Compare premium pricing at $20 vs. $30 for a 10-hour indie puzzle game. What signals justify the higher price?"*

- **Free-to-Play (F2P)** — The game costs nothing to download. Revenue comes from in-game purchases. Removes the biggest barrier to entry but requires a monetization layer that doesn't alienate players.
  *"Design a F2P monetization model for a competitive tower defense game that avoids pay-to-win accusations."*

- **Freemium** — A hybrid: the base game is free, but premium features or content cost money. Common in mobile. The free tier is the demo; the paid tier is the real game.
  *"What should the free tier of my freemium city builder include to hook players without giving away the full experience?"*

- **Subscription** — Players pay recurring fees for access. Works for live-service games with steady content drops. The promise: always something new. The risk: content treadmill.
  *"Is a $5/month subscription viable for an indie multiplayer game with a 10K player base? What content cadence would justify it?"*

- **Pay-What-You-Want** — Let players choose their price. Often used in bundles or charity events. Surprisingly effective when paired with social proof and suggested amounts.
  *"Design a pay-what-you-want launch for my narrative game. What suggested price tiers and incentives would maximize revenue?"*

- **Early Access Pricing** — Selling an unfinished game at a discount, with the price rising as development progresses. Players get a deal; developers get funding. Trust is the currency.
  *"Should I price my Early Access survival game at $15 and raise to $25 at 1.0, or start at $20? What are the tradeoffs?"*

- **Regional Pricing** — Adjusting prices by country based on local purchasing power. Steam recommends it. Ignoring it means locking out entire markets.
  *"Set up regional pricing for my $20 game across Steam's recommended tiers. Which regions need the steepest discounts?"*
  [Visual reference](https://www.google.com/search?tbm=isch&q=steam+regional+pricing+map+recommended)

- **Price Anchoring** — Setting a reference price that makes the actual price feel like a deal. A $50 "Deluxe Edition" makes the $30 "Standard" look reasonable.
  *"Design three edition tiers (Standard, Deluxe, Ultimate) for my RPG where the middle tier captures 60% of sales."*

- **Launch Discount** — A temporary price cut at release to drive initial sales velocity. Common on Steam. Too deep and you devalue the game; too shallow and it doesn't move the needle.
  *"What launch discount percentage maximizes first-week units on Steam without training customers to wait for sales?"*

- **Price Elasticity** — How sensitive demand is to price changes. Some games sell the same at $15 or $20. Others lose half their audience for every $5 increase.
  *"My roguelike sells 500 units/month at $15. If I raise to $20, how do I estimate the impact on revenue using price elasticity?"*

### In-Game Purchases

What players buy after they've already downloaded the game.

- **Microtransaction** — Any small in-game purchase. The building block of F2P revenue. Can range from $0.99 color swaps to $99.99 currency packs.
  *"Design a microtransaction catalog for a casual puzzle game that generates revenue without breaking progression."*

- **Cosmetic MTX** — Purchases that change appearance but not gameplay. Skins, emotes, trails. The gold standard of "ethical" monetization because they don't affect competitive balance.
  *"What cosmetic categories would players pay for in a top-down co-op shooter? Rank them by likely revenue."*
  [Visual reference](https://www.google.com/search?tbm=isch&q=cosmetic+microtransaction+skin+game+store)

- **Loot Box** — A randomized purchase where the player doesn't know what they'll get. Controversial, regulated in some countries, and incredibly effective at generating revenue through variable reward psychology.
  *"Design an alternative to loot boxes that preserves the excitement of random rewards without the gambling controversy."*

- **Battle Pass** — A tiered reward track with free and premium tiers, progressed through play over a set season. Players pay for the premium track. Drives both revenue and engagement.
  *"Structure a 10-week battle pass for my multiplayer game with 50 free and 50 premium tiers. What reward pacing keeps players grinding?"*
  [Visual reference](https://www.google.com/search?tbm=isch&q=battle+pass+reward+track+tier+structure)

- **Gacha** — A monetization system rooted in Japanese capsule-toy machines. Players spend currency for random character or item pulls. Drives massive revenue through collection mechanics and pity systems.
  *"Design a gacha system with a pity mechanic that guarantees a top-tier character within 90 pulls. What's the expected spend?"*

- **Virtual Currency** — In-game money purchased with real money. Creates psychological distance from real spending. 1000 Gems costs $9.99, and that skin costs 800 Gems — players stop thinking in dollars.
  *"Should my game use a single virtual currency or two (premium + earned)? What are the monetization tradeoffs?"*

- **Whale** — A player who spends far more than average. In many F2P games, 1-2% of players generate 50%+ of revenue. Designing for whales means creating deep spending sinks without exploiting vulnerable players.
  *"How do I design monetization that serves whales without making free players feel like second-class citizens?"*

- **Conversion Funnel** — The path from free player to first purchase. Each step (awareness → interest → intent → purchase) loses players. Optimizing the funnel means removing friction at every stage.
  *"Map the conversion funnel for my F2P mobile game. Where are players most likely dropping off before their first purchase?"*

- **First-Time Buyer Offer** — A deeply discounted introductory purchase designed to break the spending barrier. Once a player buys once, they're far more likely to buy again.
  *"Design a $0.99 starter pack for my RPG that feels like a must-buy without giving away too much value."*

- **Pay-to-Win** — When purchases provide competitive advantages. The fastest way to kill goodwill in a multiplayer game. Even the perception of pay-to-win can be fatal.
  *"Audit my game's shop items. Which ones cross the line from convenience into pay-to-win, and how do I fix them?"*

### Post-Launch Revenue

How games keep making money after release day.

- **DLC (Downloadable Content)** — Paid add-ons released after launch. Can range from a costume pack to a full story expansion. The question is always: should this have been in the base game?
  *"Plan three DLC releases for my adventure game over 12 months. What content justifies $5, $10, and $15 price points?"*

- **Season Pass** — A bundle that grants access to all DLC in a defined period, usually at a discount. Locks in revenue early but commits you to a content schedule.
  *"Price a season pass that covers four quarterly DLC packs. What discount over buying individually makes it compelling?"*

- **Expansion** — A major content release that significantly extends the game. New areas, mechanics, story chapters. Think Witcher 3: Blood and Wine — practically a new game.
  *"Scope an expansion for my base-building game. How much new content justifies a $20 standalone expansion vs. $10 DLC?"*

- **Bundle** — Selling multiple games or DLC together at a discount. Platform bundles (Humble, Fanatical) reach new audiences. Your own bundles upsell existing players.
  *"Should I bundle my three-game series at 60% off, or would that cannibalize sales of the latest entry?"*

- **Sale Event** — Timed discounts tied to platform events (Steam Summer Sale) or self-created events. The revenue spike can be massive, but over-discounting trains players to never buy at full price.
  *"Plan a sale calendar for my first year on Steam. Which seasonal sales should I participate in, and at what discount depth?"*
  [Visual reference](https://www.google.com/search?tbm=isch&q=steam+sale+revenue+spike+graph+indie)

- **Long Tail** — Revenue that continues trickling in months or years after launch. Most indie games make the majority of their lifetime revenue after the first month, driven by sales, word of mouth, and updates.
  *"My game sold 5K units in month one. What strategies maximize long-tail revenue over the next two years?"*

- **Catalog Revenue** — Income from a studio's back catalog of previously released games. Each new release can drive discovery of older titles. A growing catalog becomes a compounding asset.
  *"How do I cross-promote my new game to buyers of my previous three titles without being spammy?"*

- **Remaster/Port** — Re-releasing a game on new platforms or with updated graphics. Relatively low development cost for a fresh revenue window. Switch ports of indie games have been goldmines.
  *"Estimate the effort and revenue potential of porting my PC indie game to Nintendo Switch vs. mobile."*

- **Sequel Premium** — The ability to charge more for a sequel because the original built an audience. Sequel buyers are pre-sold on the concept — they just need to see improvement.
  *"My first game sold 50K units at $15. Can I price the sequel at $25? What improvements justify the increase?"*

- **Cross-Sell** — Promoting one product to buyers of another. Players who loved your roguelike might love your tactics game. Every game you ship grows your addressable audience.
  *"Design an in-game cross-sell for my back catalog that appears after credits roll. How do I make it feel like a recommendation, not an ad?"*

## Marketing & Visibility

How games get found, build audiences, and convert interest into sales.

### Store Page & Wishlists

Your store page is your storefront. Most purchasing decisions happen here.

- **Capsule Art** — The small rectangular image that represents your game in store listings, search results, and recommendations. It's the first thing players see. If it doesn't pop at thumbnail size, nothing else matters.
  *"Design capsule art for my pixel-art metroidvania that stands out in a Steam search grid. What composition rules work at 231x87?"*
  [Visual reference](https://www.google.com/search?tbm=isch&q=steam+capsule+art+game+store+listing+examples)

- **Store Page** — The full product page on a digital storefront. Screenshots, description, trailers, reviews, tags — all working together to convert a browser into a buyer.
  *"Audit my Steam store page. What elements above the fold need improvement to increase conversion from visit to wishlist?"*

- **Wishlist** — A player bookmarking your game for later. On Steam, wishlists drive algorithm visibility, sale notifications, and launch-day conversions. More wishlists = more eyeballs.
  *"My game has 8K wishlists before launch. What conversion rate to sales should I expect, and how do I improve it?"*

- **Conversion Rate** — The percentage of store page visitors who take an action (wishlist, purchase). Industry average on Steam is roughly 7-15% visit-to-wishlist. Every element on your page affects this number.
  *"My store page gets 1K daily visits but only 3% wishlist. What changes would push that closer to 10%?"*

- **Above the Fold** — The content visible without scrolling. On a store page, this is your header image, short description, and first screenshot. If these don't hook immediately, players bounce.
  *"Rewrite my game's short description for the above-the-fold section. It needs to communicate genre, hook, and tone in two sentences."*

- **Tags & Categories** — Labels that classify your game for search and recommendation algorithms. Choosing the right tags determines who sees your game. Wrong tags attract the wrong audience and tank reviews.
  *"What Steam tags should I use for a cozy roguelike with farming elements? How do I balance niche accuracy with broad discoverability?"*

- **Social Proof** — Evidence that other people like your game. Reviews, player counts, awards, press quotes. Humans follow the crowd — social proof converts fence-sitters.
  *"My game has 50 reviews at 92% positive. How do I leverage this social proof on my store page and in marketing materials?"*

- **Call to Action** — The specific thing you want a visitor to do. "Wishlist Now," "Play the Demo," "Buy in Early Access." Every piece of marketing needs a clear, single CTA.
  *"Write three variations of a call-to-action for my Coming Soon page that drives wishlists."*

- **Coming Soon Page** — A store page published before the game launches. Starts collecting wishlists and building visibility months before release. The earlier it's up, the longer it works for you.
  *"When should I publish my Coming Soon page relative to my planned launch date? What's the minimum viable page content?"*

- **Steam Algorithm** — The recommendation and visibility systems that determine which games Steam shows to which players. Driven by wishlists, sales velocity, playtime, and review scores. Understanding it is free marketing.
  *"How does the Steam discovery algorithm weight wishlists vs. first-week sales vs. review score for featuring on the front page?"*

### Community Building

Growing an audience that cares about your game before, during, and after launch.

- **Discord Server** — A community hub where players gather, chat, and provide feedback. For indie devs, it's a direct line to your most engaged fans. Quality of conversation matters more than member count.
  *"Set up a Discord server structure for my upcoming indie game. What channels drive engagement without creating noise?"*

- **Devlog** — A development diary shared publicly. Shows progress, builds trust, and gives fans a reason to keep coming back. The best devlogs make development itself entertaining.
  *"Write a devlog post about implementing my new combat system that's engaging for both developers and non-technical fans."*
  [Visual reference](https://www.google.com/search?tbm=isch&q=indie+game+devlog+post+steam+example)

- **Social Media Presence** — Your game's footprint across Twitter/X, TikTok, Reddit, YouTube, and other platforms. Each platform has different content norms. What works on TikTok dies on Twitter.
  *"Create a weekly social media content plan for my solo dev project. What content types perform best on each platform?"*

- **Content Creator** — Anyone who makes videos, streams, or articles about games. They're the modern word-of-mouth engine. One popular creator can move more units than a billboard.
  *"Identify the right tier of content creators for my niche strategy game. Should I target 10K or 100K subscriber channels?"*

- **Demo** — A free, playable slice of your game. Demos drive wishlists, generate feedback, and give streamers content. Steam Next Fest has made demos almost mandatory for indie launches.
  *"Scope a 30-minute demo for my RPG that showcases the core loop without spoiling the best content. What section of the game works best?"*

- **Playtest** — A structured testing session where outside players try your game and provide feedback. Can be closed (NDA'd) or open (public beta). The earlier you playtest, the cheaper it is to fix problems.
  *"Organize a closed playtest for 50 players. What feedback mechanisms capture useful data without overwhelming testers?"*

- **Newsletter** — An email list of interested players. You own this audience — no algorithm can take it away. Even a small list (1K subscribers) is more reliable than 10K social media followers.
  *"Write a pre-launch newsletter drip sequence: signup confirmation, monthly update, launch announcement. What drives opens and clicks?"*

- **Community Manager** — A person (or role) dedicated to engaging with the player community. Answers questions, gathers feedback, moderates discussions, and keeps the vibe positive. In small studios, the dev is the CM.
  *"I'm a solo dev managing my own community. What daily/weekly routines keep the Discord and socials healthy in under an hour a day?"*

- **User-Generated Content (UGC)** — Content created by players: mods, levels, fan art, guides. UGC extends your game's life and builds community investment. Steam Workshop makes it frictionless.
  *"Add mod support to my puzzle game. What tools and documentation do modders need to create and share custom levels?"*

- **Word of Mouth** — Players recommending your game to others. The most powerful and cheapest marketing channel. You can't buy it, but you can design for it by creating share-worthy moments.
  *"What moments in my game are most likely to generate word-of-mouth sharing? How do I amplify them?"*

### Press & Influencers

Getting coverage from media and content creators who can amplify your reach.

- **Press Kit** — A downloadable package of assets: logos, screenshots, trailers, descriptions, and key facts. Makes a journalist's job easy. If you don't have one, you won't get covered.
  *"Build a press kit for my indie game using presskit.html. What assets and information do journalists actually use?"*

- **Key Distribution** — Sending free game keys to press, influencers, and curators. Use platforms like Keymailer or Woovit to manage requests. Track who you've sent keys to and what coverage resulted.
  *"Plan key distribution for my launch. How many keys should I allocate for press vs. influencers vs. curators?"*

- **Embargo** — An agreement that recipients of review copies won't publish coverage until a specified date. Coordinates a wave of simultaneous reviews at launch. Embargoes signal confidence.
  *"Set an embargo for my game's reviews to lift the day before launch. What's the standard communication for embargo terms?"*

- **Influencer Marketing** — Partnering with content creators to promote your game, either through paid sponsorships or free key distribution. Authenticity matters — audiences can smell a forced promotion.
  *"Budget $2K for influencer marketing. Should I pay one 500K-subscriber YouTuber or give free keys to fifty 10K-subscriber creators?"*

- **Review Copy** — A free copy of the game sent to press or influencers before launch for review purposes. Timing matters: send too late and reviews miss launch; send too early and builds may be buggy.
  *"When should I send review copies relative to my launch date? What build state is 'good enough' for reviewers?"*

- **Coverage** — Any media mention of your game: articles, videos, streams, podcasts, social posts. Not all coverage is equal — a five-minute gameplay video drives more wishlists than a news blurb.
  *"Track coverage of my game launch. Which types of media coverage correlated most with wishlist and sales spikes?"*

- **Pitch Email** — The email you send to press and influencers to convince them to cover your game. Most get hundreds per week. You have one subject line and two sentences to hook them.
  *"Write a pitch email for my game that a busy journalist would actually read. Keep it under 150 words with a clear hook."*

- **Media List** — A curated spreadsheet of journalists, YouTubers, streamers, and podcasters who cover games like yours. Built through research, not purchased. Quality over quantity.
  *"Build a media list of 50 outlets and creators who cover indie roguelikes. What columns should I track beyond name and email?"*

- **Streamer Economy** — The ecosystem where streamers drive game sales through live gameplay. A game that's fun to watch sells itself on Twitch. Designing for "streamability" is a legitimate marketing strategy.
  *"What features make my game more fun to watch on Twitch? How do I design moments that create clips?"*

- **Curator** — On Steam, curators are accounts that recommend games to their followers. Curator reviews appear on your store page. Getting on the right curator lists adds passive social proof.
  *"Identify 20 Steam curators who review games in my genre. What's the best way to reach out for a review?"*

### Trailers & Creative

The visual assets that sell your game before anyone plays it.

- **Reveal Trailer** — The first public look at your game. Sets tone, genre, and hook. Doesn't need to show polished gameplay — it needs to make people feel something and want to know more.
  *"Script a 60-second reveal trailer for my horror game. What beats create intrigue without showing too much?"*

- **Gameplay Trailer** — A trailer showing actual gameplay, ideally with real player input. Players are skeptical of cinematic trailers — gameplay footage builds trust. This is your most important marketing asset.
  *"Structure a 90-second gameplay trailer that demonstrates my core loop, art style, and one 'wow' moment."*
  [Visual reference](https://www.google.com/search?tbm=isch&q=indie+game+gameplay+trailer+storyboard+beats)

- **Hook** — The first 3-5 seconds of any video content. On social media, you have under three seconds before someone scrolls. The hook must be visually arresting or emotionally provocative.
  *"Design five different hooks for my game trailer that stop a scrolling thumb in under three seconds."*

- **Beat** — A distinct moment or shift in a trailer. Action beat, emotional beat, comedy beat. Good trailers alternate beats to maintain rhythm and prevent monotony.
  *"Map out the beats for a two-minute launch trailer: opening hook, gameplay showcase, story tease, feature highlights, call to action."*

- **Thumbnail** — The preview image for a video on YouTube, social media, or a storefront. Competes for clicks against dozens of other thumbnails. High contrast, readable text, and clear focal points win.
  *"Design a YouTube thumbnail for my devlog that gets clicks. What composition and text treatment works at small sizes?"*

- **GIF Marketing** — Using short animated loops to showcase your game on social media, forums, and store pages. GIFs autoplay, require no click, and convey motion instantly. The workhorse of indie marketing.
  *"Capture five GIFs from my game that each showcase one mechanic in under 5 seconds. What moments read best as loops?"*
  [Visual reference](https://www.google.com/search?tbm=isch&q=indie+game+gif+marketing+social+media)

- **Screenshot Composition** — How you frame and stage screenshots for maximum impact. Good screenshots show action, convey mood, and communicate mechanics without explanation.
  *"Compose five store page screenshots that tell a visual story about my game's progression from early to late game."*

- **Key Art** — A single hero image that represents your game everywhere: store pages, social media headers, press kits, merchandise. It's your game's face. Invest accordingly.
  *"Brief an artist on key art for my sci-fi tactics game. What elements must it include to communicate genre and tone at a glance?"*

- **Mood Board** — A collage of reference images that defines the visual direction of your game's marketing. Aligns the team (and external collaborators) on aesthetic goals before production begins.
  *"Create a mood board for my dark fantasy game's marketing campaign. What visual themes unify the trailer, store page, and social content?"*

- **Brand Identity** — The consistent visual and tonal language across all your game's touchpoints: logo, colors, typography, voice. Strong brand identity makes your game recognizable anywhere.
  *"Define a brand identity guide for my cozy farming game: logo usage, color palette, font choices, and tone of voice for social media."*

## Publishing & Distribution

How games reach players — platforms, deals, and launch logistics.

### Platform Economics

The financial structures of the stores and platforms where games are sold.

- **Revenue Split** — The percentage of each sale that goes to the developer vs. the platform. Steam's default is 70/30. Epic is 88/12. The split directly impacts your per-unit profit.
  *"Calculate my net revenue per unit on Steam (70/30) vs. Epic (88/12) for a $20 game after regional pricing and refunds."*

- **Platform Fee** — The cut a platform takes from every sale. Includes payment processing, hosting, and discovery. Some fees are negotiable at scale. Most indie devs pay the standard rate.
  *"Compare platform fees across Steam, Epic, itch.io, and GOG for a $15 indie game. Where do I net the most per sale?"*

- **Exclusivity Deal** — An agreement to release on one platform only, usually in exchange for guaranteed revenue, marketing support, or an upfront payment. Trade audience reach for financial security.
  *"Epic is offering a six-month exclusivity deal with a $50K minimum guarantee. My expected first-year Steam revenue is $80K. Should I take it?"*

- **Cross-Platform** — Releasing on multiple platforms (PC, console, mobile) or enabling play across them. Expands audience but multiplies development and QA cost.
  *"Prioritize platform launches for my indie game: PC first, then which console? What factors determine the order?"*

- **Console Certification** — The approval process required to publish on PlayStation, Xbox, or Nintendo Switch. Each platform has technical requirements, content policies, and submission timelines.
  *"What are the common certification failure points for indie games on Nintendo Switch, and how do I avoid them?"*

- **First-Party** — Games published by the platform holder (Nintendo, Sony, Microsoft). First-party titles get preferential marketing and placement. Understanding first-party strategy helps you predict platform trends.
  *"How do first-party release schedules on Switch affect indie game visibility? When are the best windows to launch?"*

- **Third-Party** — Any publisher or developer that isn't the platform holder. That's you. Third-party developers navigate platform rules, fees, and discovery systems that first-party titles bypass.
  *"What third-party developer programs (Xbox ID@Xbox, PlayStation Partners, Nintendo Indie World) should I apply to, and what do they offer?"*

- **Digital Storefront** — Any online store that sells games: Steam, Epic, GOG, itch.io, Humble Store, console stores. Each has different audiences, features, fee structures, and discovery mechanics.
  *"Rank digital storefronts by indie game revenue potential. Where should I invest time setting up store pages beyond Steam?"*

- **Retail vs. Digital** — Physical copies vs. digital downloads. Digital dominates (95%+ of PC sales), but physical editions serve collectors, generate press, and work as marketing. Limited Run Games built a business on this.
  *"Is a limited physical edition worth producing for my indie game? What's the minimum viable run size and expected margin?"*

- **Platform Holder** — The company that owns and operates a gaming platform: Valve (Steam), Apple (App Store), Nintendo, Sony, Microsoft. They set the rules, take the fees, and control the algorithms.
  *"How do platform holder policies on pricing, discounts, and key generation affect my ability to run promotions?"*

### Publishing Deals

The business relationships between developers and publishers.

- **Publisher** — A company that funds, markets, and/or distributes a game in exchange for a share of revenue. Ranges from massive (EA, Ubisoft) to boutique indie labels (Devolver, Annapurna, Raw Fury).
  *"What should I look for in an indie publisher? List the top 10 deal terms to evaluate before signing."*
  [Visual reference](https://www.google.com/search?tbm=isch&q=indie+game+publisher+logos+devolver+annapurna+raw+fury)

- **Minimum Guarantee (MG)** — The minimum amount a publisher guarantees the developer will earn, regardless of sales. If the game underperforms, you keep the MG. If it overperforms, you earn beyond it.
  *"A publisher offers a $100K MG against a 70/30 rev share in their favor. How many units at $20 do I need to sell before I earn beyond the MG?"*

- **Advance** — Upfront money from a publisher to fund development. Usually recoupable — meaning the publisher recoups the advance from your share of revenue before you see additional earnings.
  *"I need $200K to finish my game. What advance terms are standard for a first-time dev with a strong demo and 15K wishlists?"*

- **Revenue Share** — The split of post-launch revenue between developer and publisher. Typical indie deals range from 60/40 to 80/20 in the developer's favor, depending on what the publisher provides.
  *"Compare a 70/30 rev share with full marketing support vs. self-publishing with 100% revenue. At what sales volume does self-publishing win?"*

- **Recoup** — The process of a publisher earning back their advance from the developer's share of revenue. Until the advance is recouped, the developer receives reduced (or zero) royalty payments.
  *"My publisher advanced $150K with a 50/50 rev share post-recoup. If the game earns $400K gross, what's my total take-home?"*

- **IP Ownership** — Who owns the intellectual property — the game's characters, world, code, and brand. The most important term in any publishing deal. Losing IP ownership means losing control of your own creation.
  *"A publisher wants IP co-ownership in exchange for a larger advance. What are the long-term risks, and what alternatives should I negotiate?"*

- **First Right of Refusal** — A clause giving the publisher the first opportunity to publish your next game. Common in deals. Limits your freedom but can mean a smoother path for your follow-up project.
  *"My publishing contract includes first right of refusal on my next two games. How do I negotiate this down or add protections?"*

- **Milestone Payment** — Development funding released in installments tied to hitting specific deliverables (alpha, beta, release candidate). Aligns incentives: publisher pays for progress, developer proves capability.
  *"Structure a milestone payment schedule for a 24-month development cycle. What deliverables make sense at each gate?"*

- **Dev-Pub Agreement** — The formal contract between developer and publisher. Covers everything: funding, revenue split, IP, milestones, marketing responsibilities, termination clauses. Get a lawyer.
  *"What are the top five red flags in a dev-pub agreement that an indie developer should watch for?"*

- **Self-Publishing** — Releasing your game without a publisher. You keep all revenue and control but handle everything: funding, marketing, QA, localization, platform relations, customer support.
  *"Create a self-publishing checklist for launching on Steam. What tasks does a publisher normally handle that I now own?"*

### Launch Strategy

The decisions and timing that determine how your game enters the market.

- **Early Access** — Releasing a playable but unfinished game, with plans to develop it further based on player feedback and continued funding. A development model, a funding model, and a marketing risk all in one.
  *"Should my survival game launch in Early Access or wait for 1.0? What criteria help me decide?"*

- **Launch Window** — The specific period you target for release. Avoid AAA launch months, major holidays (when press is offline), and oversaturated sale periods. Timing can make or break visibility.
  *"Identify the best launch window for my indie RPG in the next 12 months. What competitor releases and events should I avoid?"*

- **Day-One Patch** — A software update available on launch day to fix issues found after the game went gold or through certification. Expected for console releases. Shouldn't be a crutch for shipping broken.
  *"My game went to cert with three known bugs. What's the timeline for getting a day-one patch approved on PlayStation?"*

- **Soft Launch** — Releasing in a limited market (one region or platform) to test and optimize before a full global launch. Common in mobile. Lets you fix problems before the whole world is watching.
  *"Plan a soft launch in Canada and Australia for my mobile game. What metrics determine readiness for global launch?"*

- **Staggered Release** — Launching on different platforms at different times. PC first, then consoles months later. Each launch becomes its own marketing moment, but you risk losing cross-platform buzz.
  *"Should I stagger my launch (PC → Switch → PlayStation) or go simultaneous? What are the revenue and marketing tradeoffs?"*

- **Embargo Lift** — The moment when press and influencers are allowed to publish their reviews and coverage. Typically set 24-48 hours before launch to build hype. A well-timed lift creates a wave of simultaneous coverage.
  *"Set my review embargo to lift 48 hours before launch. How do I communicate this clearly to 100+ recipients?"*

- **Review Bombing** — A coordinated wave of negative reviews, often unrelated to game quality (e.g., political controversy, price increase, developer behavior). Platforms have systems to detect and mitigate it, but the damage can be real.
  *"My game is getting review-bombed over a controversial patch. What steps can I take to mitigate the impact and respond appropriately?"*

- **Launch Trailer Timing** — When to release your launch trailer relative to launch day. Too early and momentum fades. Too late and you miss the pre-launch excitement window. Most studios target 1-3 days before.
  *"Plan the release timing for my launch trailer, social media push, and press embargo lift for a Tuesday launch."*

- **Post-Launch Roadmap** — A public timeline of planned updates, features, and content after launch. Sets player expectations, builds long-term engagement, and signals that the game isn't abandoned.
  *"Design a 6-month post-launch roadmap for my strategy game. What level of detail builds trust without over-promising?"*
  [Visual reference](https://www.google.com/search?tbm=isch&q=indie+game+post+launch+roadmap+infographic)

- **Games-as-a-Service (GaaS)** — A model where the game is continuously updated with new content, often supported by recurring purchases or subscriptions. The opposite of "ship it and move on."
  *"Is GaaS viable for a 3-person indie team? What's the minimum content cadence to keep a live-service game alive?"*

## Analytics & Growth

How games sustain, grow, and optimize after release.

### Player Metrics

The numbers that tell you how players actually behave in your game.

- **DAU/MAU** — Daily Active Users / Monthly Active Users. The ratio (DAU/MAU) measures stickiness. A ratio of 0.5 means half your monthly players play every day — that's exceptional.
  *"My game has 10K MAU and 1.5K DAU. Is this DAU/MAU ratio healthy for a single-player roguelike, or should I be concerned?"*

- **Retention Rate** — The percentage of players who return after a given period. The single most important metric for any game that wants to sustain revenue beyond launch week.
  *"My D1 retention is 40% but D7 drops to 12%. What in-game changes typically improve week-one retention?"*

- **Churn** — The rate at which players stop playing. The inverse of retention. High churn means something is pushing players away — difficulty spikes, content gaps, or bugs.
  *"Identify the top three churn points in my game using session data. Where are players quitting and never coming back?"*

- **Session Length** — How long a player plays in a single sitting. Varies by genre: mobile puzzle (5 min), PC RPG (90 min). Session length informs monetization timing and content pacing.
  *"My average session length is 8 minutes for a game designed for 20-minute sessions. What's causing players to leave early?"*

- **ARPU** — Average Revenue Per User. Total revenue divided by total users. Tells you how much each player is worth on average. Critical for calculating marketing budgets.
  *"My F2P game's ARPU is $0.50. Is this competitive for a casual mobile puzzle game, and how do I increase it?"*

- **LTV (Lifetime Value)** — The total revenue a player generates over their entire relationship with your game. LTV must exceed CPI (Cost Per Install) or you're losing money on every acquired player.
  *"Calculate the LTV of my mobile game players using 90-day retention curves and ARPU. How do I project beyond 90 days?"*

- **Cohort Analysis** — Grouping players by when they joined (install week, update version) and tracking their behavior over time. Reveals whether changes improve or harm the player experience.
  *"Compare the D30 retention of players who joined before and after my difficulty rebalance patch. Did it help?"*
  [Visual reference](https://www.google.com/search?tbm=isch&q=cohort+analysis+retention+curve+game+analytics)

- **Funnel Analysis** — Tracking player progression through sequential steps (tutorial → first level → first purchase → day 7). Each step has a dropout rate. The biggest drop reveals your biggest problem.
  *"Build a funnel from install to first purchase in my F2P game. Where is the biggest dropout, and what hypotheses explain it?"*

- **D1/D7/D30 Retention** — Industry-standard retention checkpoints: Day 1, Day 7, Day 30 after install. Benchmarks vary by genre, but roughly: D1 > 40%, D7 > 15%, D30 > 5% is healthy for mobile.
  *"My D1 is 45%, D7 is 20%, D30 is 3%. The D7-to-D30 cliff suggests a content gap — what should I investigate?"*

- **Engagement Loop** — The repeating cycle that keeps players coming back: trigger → action → reward → investment. Distinct from core loop — engagement loops operate at the session and meta level.
  *"Map the engagement loops in my idle game. What triggers bring players back, and where does the loop break?"*

### User Acquisition

How you get new players — and how much it costs.

- **CPI (Cost Per Install)** — How much you pay in advertising to acquire one new player. Varies wildly by platform and genre: $0.50 for casual mobile, $3+ for mid-core. Must be less than LTV.
  *"My CPI is $1.20 and my LTV is $1.00. I'm losing $0.20 per user. Do I fix monetization, cut ad spend, or optimize creatives?"*

- **CPA (Cost Per Action)** — The cost to get a player to take a specific action beyond installing: first purchase, reaching level 10, subscribing. More expensive than CPI but measures higher-value users.
  *"Set up a CPA campaign targeting players who complete my game's tutorial. What bid should I start with for Facebook ads?"*

- **ROAS (Return on Ad Spend)** — Revenue generated per dollar spent on advertising. A ROAS of 1.5 means every $1 in ads produces $1.50 in revenue. Below 1.0 means you're losing money.
  *"My ROAS on TikTok ads is 0.8 but on YouTube it's 2.1. How should I reallocate my $5K monthly ad budget?"*

- **Organic vs. Paid** — Organic users find your game naturally (search, word of mouth, featuring). Paid users come from advertising. Organic is free but unpredictable. Paid is controllable but costs money.
  *"70% of my installs are paid. How do I shift the balance toward organic through better ASO and content marketing?"*

- **Install Attribution** — Tracking which ad, campaign, or channel led to each install. Essential for knowing where to spend. Tools like AppsFlyer, Adjust, or Singular handle this.
  *"Set up install attribution for my mobile game. What SDK and attribution windows should I configure?"*

- **Ad Creative** — The actual advertisement: video, image, or playable ad. Creative quality is the single biggest lever in user acquisition. A great creative can cut CPI in half overnight.
  *"Design three ad creative concepts for my puzzle game. What hooks and gameplay moments convert best in 15-second video ads?"*
  [Visual reference](https://www.google.com/search?tbm=isch&q=mobile+game+ad+creative+video+examples)

- **Lookalike Audience** — A targeting strategy where ad platforms find users similar to your existing players. Upload your best players' data; the algorithm finds more people like them.
  *"Create a lookalike audience from my top 10% spenders. What seed audience size gives the best balance of quality and reach?"*

- **Cross-Promotion** — Advertising one of your games inside another. Free traffic between your own titles. Works best when games share an audience or aesthetic.
  *"Design an in-game cross-promotion for my new game that appears in my existing game's main menu. What placement converts best?"*

- **Viral Coefficient** — The average number of new players each existing player brings in. A coefficient above 1.0 means exponential growth. Below 1.0 and growth requires external fuel (ads, PR).
  *"My game's viral coefficient is 0.3. What social features or referral mechanics could push it above 0.5?"*

- **K-Factor** — A measure of viral growth: (invites sent per user) x (conversion rate per invite). K-Factor > 1 means organic exponential growth. Rarely achieved, but even K=0.5 significantly reduces CPI.
  *"Calculate my game's K-Factor. Each player invites 3 friends, and 10% of invitees install. What does this mean for growth?"*

### Live Operations

Managing and evolving a game after launch to sustain engagement and revenue.

- **Content Cadence** — The rhythm of new content releases. Weekly challenges, monthly updates, quarterly expansions. Consistent cadence trains players to return. Inconsistency kills habit formation.
  *"Plan a content cadence for my live-service game with a 3-person team. What's sustainable without burning out?"*

- **Seasonal Event** — A time-limited in-game event tied to real-world seasons or holidays. Halloween skins, winter festivals, anniversary celebrations. Creates urgency and a reason to return.
  *"Design a Halloween seasonal event for my multiplayer game. What exclusive rewards drive participation without alienating players who miss it?"*

- **Limited-Time Offer** — A purchase available only for a short window. Creates urgency through scarcity. Effective for monetization but can feel manipulative if overused.
  *"Structure a weekend-only limited-time offer that drives 3x daily revenue without making regular-priced items feel like bad deals."*

- **FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)** — The anxiety players feel about missing limited content or events. A powerful motivator that, used ethically, drives engagement. Used excessively, it creates resentment and burnout.
  *"My seasonal events create FOMO that's driving negative reviews. How do I maintain urgency while respecting players' time?"*

- **Server-Side Config** — Game settings that can be changed without shipping a client update. Prices, event schedules, difficulty tuning, feature flags. Enables rapid iteration and live balancing.
  *"What game variables should I make server-configurable at launch to enable live ops without client patches?"*

- **A/B Testing** — Showing different versions of content, UI, or pricing to different player groups and measuring which performs better. The scientific method applied to game design decisions.
  *"Set up an A/B test for two different tutorial flows. What sample size and duration give statistically significant results?"*

- **Feature Flag** — A toggle that enables or disables a feature for specific player segments without deploying new code. Essential for gradual rollouts, testing, and quick kill switches.
  *"Implement feature flags for my new social system so I can roll it out to 10% of players first and monitor for issues."*

- **Player Segmentation** — Dividing your player base into groups based on behavior, spending, tenure, or engagement. Allows targeted messaging, offers, and content. Treating all players the same leaves money on the table.
  *"Segment my players into new, engaged, at-risk, and lapsed. What re-engagement strategies work for each segment?"*

- **Push Notification** — A message sent to a player's device to pull them back into the game. Timing, frequency, and relevance determine whether it's helpful or annoying. Over-notification drives uninstalls.
  *"Design a push notification strategy that re-engages lapsed players without annoying active ones. What messages and timing work best?"*

- **Win-Back Campaign** — A targeted effort to re-engage players who have stopped playing. Usually involves special offers, content updates, or personalized messages. Cheaper than acquiring new players.
  *"Launch a win-back campaign for players who haven't logged in for 30+ days. What incentive brings them back without devaluing the game?"*

## Funding & Finance

How games get funded and how studios stay alive.

### Funding Sources

Where the money comes from to make a game.

- **Bootstrapping** — Funding development from personal savings or revenue from previous projects. Full control, no external obligations. The constraint: your runway is your bank account.
  *"I have $40K in savings and 12 months of living expenses. Can I bootstrap a solo game project, and what scope should I target?"*

- **Crowdfunding** — Raising money from the public through platforms like Kickstarter or Indiegogo. Pre-sells the game and validates demand. But a failed campaign is public, and fulfillment is a second job.
  *"Plan a Kickstarter campaign for my tactical RPG. What funding goal, reward tiers, and campaign length maximize success odds?"*
  [Visual reference](https://www.google.com/search?tbm=isch&q=kickstarter+indie+game+campaign+page+reward+tiers)

- **Angel Investor** — An individual who invests personal money in early-stage companies. Offers smaller amounts than VCs but with fewer strings. Often provides mentorship alongside capital.
  *"How do I find angel investors interested in indie games? What pitch materials do they expect, and what deal terms are standard?"*

- **Venture Capital (VC)** — Institutional funding from firms that invest in high-growth companies. VCs want 10x+ returns, which rarely aligns with indie game economics. More relevant for studios building platforms or live-service games.
  *"A VC wants to invest $500K for 20% equity. What growth expectations come with that, and is it realistic for an indie studio?"*

- **Publisher Advance** — Money from a publisher to fund development, recouped from future sales. The most common external funding for indie games. See [Publishing Deals](#publishing-deals) for details.
  *"Compare a $200K publisher advance (recoupable, 70/30 rev share) vs. a $100K grant (non-recoupable). Which is better for a game expected to earn $500K?"*

- **Grant** — Non-repayable funding from governments, arts councils, or organizations. Epic MegaGrants, Canada Media Fund, Nordic Game Program. Free money, but competitive and slow.
  *"Which game development grants am I eligible for as a solo US-based indie developer? What do winning applications look like?"*

- **Revenue-Based Financing** — A loan repaid as a percentage of future revenue. No equity given up, no fixed monthly payments. Repayment scales with success. Providers include XSOLLA and specialized game funds.
  *"Compare revenue-based financing at 5% of gross revenue vs. a traditional loan at 8% APR for a $100K game budget."*

- **Equity** — Ownership in your company, traded for investment. Giving up 20% equity means giving up 20% of all future profits and decision-making power. Equity is expensive — only trade it for transformative capital.
  *"An investor wants 25% equity for $300K. What is my studio's implied valuation, and is this a fair deal given my track record?"*

- **Pitch Deck** — A presentation (usually 10-15 slides) that sells your game and studio to potential funders. Covers the game, market, team, financials, and ask. First impressions matter — design counts.
  *"Build a pitch deck for my indie studio seeking $200K in funding. What slides do investors expect, and in what order?"*

- **Term Sheet** — A non-binding document outlining the key terms of an investment deal before the full contract is drafted. Covers valuation, equity, board seats, and rights. The negotiation starts here.
  *"I received a term sheet from an investor. What are the top five terms I should negotiate, and what's standard for indie game studios?"*

### Financial Planning

The numbers that keep your studio alive and your project on track.

- **Runway** — How many months you can operate before running out of money. Runway = cash in bank / monthly expenses. The most important number in your studio.
  *"I have $60K in the bank and burn $5K/month. My game launches in 14 months. Do I have enough runway, and what's my margin of safety?"*

- **Burn Rate** — How much money your studio spends per month. Includes salaries, software, hardware, contractors, office space. Knowing your burn rate is step one of not dying.
  *"Calculate my monthly burn rate as a 3-person remote studio. What costs do first-time studio founders commonly forget?"*

- **Break-Even** — The point where total revenue equals total costs. After break-even, every sale is profit. Before it, you're still paying off development. A critical milestone for any commercial game.
  *"My game cost $80K to make. At $15 after Steam's cut, how many units do I need to sell to break even?"*

- **Unit Economics** — The revenue and cost associated with a single unit (one copy sold, one player acquired). If it costs $2 to acquire a player who generates $1.50 in LTV, your unit economics are broken.
  *"Break down the unit economics of my $20 Steam game: gross revenue, platform cut, taxes, refunds, and net to developer."*

- **Gross Margin** — Revenue minus cost of goods sold, expressed as a percentage. For digital games, COGS is low (hosting, payment processing), so gross margins are typically 70-90%.
  *"What's the gross margin on my game after Steam's cut, payment fees, and server costs? How does this compare to industry benchmarks?"*

- **Net Revenue** — What's left after all deductions: platform fees, taxes, refunds, publisher share. The number that actually hits your bank account. Always smaller than you expect.
  *"My game grossed $100K on Steam. Walk me through every deduction to calculate my actual net revenue."*

- **Cash Flow** — The timing of money in and money out. You can be profitable on paper but bankrupt in practice if expenses come before revenue. Cash flow management is survival.
  *"Map my studio's cash flow for the next 12 months, including a $50K publisher milestone in month 4 and launch revenue in month 10."*

- **Profit Margin** — Net profit as a percentage of revenue. After all expenses — development, marketing, platform fees, taxes — what percentage do you actually keep?
  *"My game earned $200K with $150K total costs. What's my profit margin, and how does it compare to indie game averages?"*

- **Opportunity Cost** — The value of what you give up by choosing one path over another. Every month spent on this game is a month not spent on the next one, or a job, or consulting work.
  *"I can spend 6 more months polishing my game or launch now and start my next project. How do I evaluate the opportunity cost?"*

- **Financial Projection** — A forecast of future revenue and expenses. Usually modeled as optimistic, realistic, and pessimistic scenarios. Investors and publishers expect them. Your survival may depend on their accuracy.
  *"Build a 24-month financial projection for my studio with three scenarios. What assumptions drive the biggest variance?"*
  [Visual reference](https://www.google.com/search?tbm=isch&q=indie+game+studio+financial+projection+spreadsheet)

### Studio Operations

The legal, structural, and human side of running a game studio.

- **LLC/Corp Structure** — The legal entity type for your studio. LLC offers liability protection with tax flexibility. C-Corp is standard for raising investment. Sole proprietorship is fine until it isn't.
  *"Should I form an LLC or S-Corp for my two-person indie studio? What are the tax and liability implications?"*

- **IP Ownership** — Who legally owns the game you're making. If you use contractors without proper agreements, they may own parts of your game. Work-for-hire clauses and IP assignment agreements prevent this.
  *"I hired a freelance artist without a contract. Who owns the art they created for my game, and how do I fix this?"*

- **Contract Work** — Hiring individuals or studios for specific tasks: art, music, QA, porting. Contracts should specify deliverables, timelines, payment, and IP assignment. No handshake deals.
  *"Draft key terms for a contract with a freelance composer delivering a 20-track soundtrack. What clauses protect both parties?"*

- **Work-for-Hire** — A legal arrangement where the person creating the work doesn't own it — the hiring party does. Must be explicitly stated in the contract to be enforceable.
  *"Write a work-for-hire clause for my contractor agreements that clearly assigns all IP to my studio."*

- **Royalty** — An ongoing percentage of revenue paid to a contributor. Alternative to flat-fee payment. Aligns incentives (contributor earns more if the game succeeds) but complicates accounting.
  *"My composer wants 3% royalties instead of a flat $5K fee. If my game earns $200K, which deal is better for me?"*

- **NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement)** — A legal agreement preventing someone from sharing confidential information. Standard before pitching to publishers or showing pre-announcement builds to testers.
  *"Write an NDA for playtesters who will see my unannounced game. What should it cover, and how enforceable is it?"*

- **Scope Creep Budget** — A financial buffer specifically reserved for the inevitable expansion of project scope. Because every game adds features that weren't in the original plan. Budget for it or pay for it later.
  *"I've budgeted 10% for scope creep on a $100K project. Is that enough, and how do I manage the fund when requests come in?"*

- **Contingency** — A percentage of the total budget set aside for unexpected costs: bugs, delays, hardware failures, contractor no-shows. Industry standard is 10-20%. Skip it at your peril.
  *"My game budget is $150K with no contingency. What's the right contingency percentage, and what unexpected costs commonly hit indie studios?"*

- **Overhead** — The ongoing costs of running a studio that aren't tied to a specific project: rent, software licenses, insurance, accounting. Low overhead is the indie studio's structural advantage.
  *"Audit my studio's monthly overhead. What subscriptions, tools, and costs can I cut without affecting production quality?"*

- **Team Scaling** — Growing (or shrinking) your team to match project needs. Hiring too early burns runway. Hiring too late delays the project. The art is timing it right.
  *"My solo project needs a second programmer for the final six months. Should I hire full-time, contract, or revenue-share?"*
